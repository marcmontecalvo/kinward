# PRD Quality Review — Kinward Assistant Experience

## Overall verdict

This PRD holds up well as a chain-top spec: its decision registers (§18–§19), per-requirement traceability (§17.4), and deterministic privacy/action policies (§8–§11) are genuinely strong and source-extractable for architecture and epic creation. The risks are concentrated in done-ness — several FRs encode thesis-level behavior behind unbounded words like "relevant" and "personality" — and in three user journeys that lack named protagonists, which will force story creators to infer context the PRD should carry inline. Resolve the vague FRs and name the floating UJ protagonists before marking the PRD final.

## Decision-readiness — strong

The PRD treats decisions as decisions. Sections 18 and 19 are genuine decision registers — not "considerations" or soft-balanced prose — with stable IDs (AD-01 through AD-15, PD-01 through PD-07), named owners ("Architect", "Product owner"), due milestones, affected-requirement lists, and a safe interim behavior on every row. The interim behaviors name what was given up, not just what was chosen: AD-12's "Credentials are excluded from backups and require reauthorization" states the cost explicitly; AD-10's "Shared surfaces remain household-safe with no `verified` state until resolved" surfaces the real tension between personal convenience and privacy determinism. The readiness criteria (§20) are honest about open items: "every entry ... is resolved with a recorded disposition, or explicitly accepted as open with its owner, due milestone, and safe interim behavior" (line 962). A decision-maker can act on this PRD — each open item has a fallback that keeps work unblocked or honestly blocks it.

The one structural gap is the absence of inline tension callouts. The rubric looks for `[NOTE FOR PM]` at "real tensions, not at safe checkpoints." The decision register captures tensions formally, but a story author reading FR-032 ("Teen and child policy shall implement Section 7 and be covered by automated tests") won't see inline that PD-02 ("Which child durable-fact categories are guardian-visible by default") is an unresolved blocker for that FR — they must cross-reference the register. For a chain-top PRD that is the correct contract, but inline callouts would reduce the chance of a story author silently deciding a blocked item.

### Findings

- **[medium]** Open-decision density at milestone gates (§18–§19) — Twenty-two decisions are all "Open," several due at "Milestone A start" (AD-01, AD-02, AD-06) or "Milestone B start" (AD-03, AD-04, AD-05, AD-15). AD-01 blocks "invitation, session, or recovery flows" and is due at Milestone A start, but FR-002 (bootstrap as "one recoverable operation") and FR-005 (invitation binding) are mapped to Milestone A and B respectively. The interim behaviors are honest, but a story author may not realize Milestone A's bootstrap API cannot include session/recovery until AD-01 resolves. *Fix:* Add inline `[BLOCKED BY AD-01]` or `[BLOCKED BY PD-02]` tags on the affected FRs so the blocker travels with the requirement, not only in the register.

## Substance over theater — strong

No persona theater. The PRD has no Personas section of the "four-plus personas who value things" variety. Instead, nine user journeys (§5) each carry a protagonist and an outcome, and the traceability table (§17.4) proves each UJ drives specific FRs — UJ-5 (shared display refuses private disclosure) maps to FR-011, FR-020–FR-021, FR-026, FR-029–FR-030, FR-033, FR-039, FR-044, FR-059, NFR-001, NFR-003, NFR-020, NFR-022, NFR-024. No journey is decorative.

No innovation theater. There is no "Differentiation" or "Why Kinward Wins" section written because the template had one. The product thesis is encoded in Section 1's explicit "Kinward is not" list (lines 24–31) and in the product outcomes (§2), not in a novelty claim.

No NFR theater. The performance NFRs carry product-specific thresholds: NFR-017 "within 2 seconds at p95," NFR-018 "within 500 ms at p95," NFR-020 "within 250 ms of the client receiving an identity downgrade and within 1 second of backend detection," NFR-022 "within 1 second of session expiry detection." These are measurable and tied to specific surfaces, not boilerplate. NFR-032 ("Public repository fixtures and examples shall use fictional or synthetic data only") and NFR-033 ("Current documentation shall remain separate from archived Homefront SaaS and routine-centric artifacts") are product-specific hygiene gates, not copied boilerplate.

No vision theater. Section 1 ("Purpose") is specific enough that it could not swap into another product's PRD: "Kinward is not: a multi-tenant SaaS product, a routine or alarm builder, a generic chatbot, a family surveillance system..." (lines 24–31). This is a product boundary, not a vision-statement fill-in.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The PRD has a thesis: a private, continuous assistant relationship per person that infers household logistics from durable facts rather than requiring routine construction, with enforced privacy boundaries between personal and household-shared contexts. Section 2 outcome #2 ("Infer useful household logistics from durable facts and changing conditions rather than requiring routine construction") and outcome #3 ("Coordinate information and actions without exposing one person's private memory to another person") state the bet. FR-048 ("Kinward shall detect relevant exceptions without requiring routine definitions") encodes the anti-routine thesis directly, and its source is tagged "Brief §Product Thesis" in the traceability table (line 821), confirming the thesis is tracked, not assumed.

Feature prioritization follows the thesis. The milestone arc (§16) sequences from foundation (A) → first live cross-surface slice (B) → first usable household release (C) → coordinating assistant (D) → voice (E), and the proactive-level split in FR-045 keeps nudge/interruption/autonomous delivery disabled until Milestone D — the thesis is about calm inference, not interruption, so the sequencing serves the thesis rather than front-loading flashy features.

Success metrics validate the thesis, not activity. Section 6.1 measures utility ("at least 70% of sampled assistant sessions produce a response, topic update, useful explanation, or permitted action without developer repair") and cross-surface continuity ("at least 80% of sampled cross-surface topic continuations resume without restating the initial request"), not DAU/MAU. Counter-metrics are present and thesis-aligned: Section 6.4's "Zero nudge, interruption, or autonomous-action deliveries occur during the Milestone C pilot, confirming those levels remain disabled until Milestone D delivers them" (line 264) is a counter-metric that directly guards the thesis. Section 6.5 carries the matching Milestone D counter-metrics ("No more than three non-critical proactive interruptions per person per day by default," "Fewer than 5% of autonomous actions are reversed because intent or scope was wrong").

### Findings

- **[low]** Thesis not labeled as thesis (§1, §2) — The product thesis is implicit in Section 1 and Section 2, and only discoverable as "thesis" via the traceability source tag "Brief §Product Thesis" on FR-048 (line 821). A story author who doesn't cross-reference the table may miss that FR-048 is load-bearing for the product's reason to exist. *Fix:* Add a one-sentence thesis statement at the top of Section 1 or Section 2 labeled as such, e.g., "Product thesis: Kinward replaces routine construction with durable-fact inference and enforced privacy boundaries."

## Done-ness clarity — adequate

Most FRs are testable. FR-001 ("exactly one household per deployment") is verifiable. FR-005 ("Invitation acceptance shall bind the authenticated account to the intended existing profile without silently creating a duplicate") has a clear negative test. FR-076 ("Restore shall complete atomically or stop without replacing the existing valid household state") is a precise atomicity condition. The NFRs (§15) carry explicit bounds — NFR-017 "within 2 seconds at p95," NFR-020 "within 250 ms" — and the milestone exit gates (§16) carry concrete completion conditions.

But several FRs encode their core behavior behind words that have no testable definition in this PRD. The rubric says to flag every "reasonable performance," "user-friendly," and "system handles X gracefully." The following FRs use unbounded qualifiers that a story author cannot translate into an acceptance test without guessing:

- **FR-048** (line 558): "Kinward shall detect relevant exceptions without requiring routine definitions." "Relevant exceptions" is undefined — relevant to whom, by what criteria? This is the thesis-encoding FR and it is the least testable requirement in the document.
- **FR-063** (line 579): "Kinward shall translate relevant state and actions into household language without requiring entity IDs or service syntax." "Household language" and "relevant state" are both unbounded. What utterances count? What entity-ID-free phrasing is acceptable?
- **FR-012** (line 510): "A person shall name an assistant and configure personality and interaction preferences without changing its permission boundary." "Personality and interaction preferences" is undefined — what preferences, configurable how, persisted where?
- **FR-022** (line 523): "Kinward shall distinguish confirmed facts, inferred observations, transient context, and household-shared facts." Distinguish how — in the UI? In the data model? Both? The verification column says "unit" but what unit test proves "distinguish"?
- **FR-008** (line 503): "A person shall be able to review and correct their profile information after onboarding." Which profile fields are reviewable/correctable is not stated.

The verification column in §17.4 names the method (unit, integration, privacy-test, e2e, etc.) but not the acceptance condition. For a chain-top PRD that feeds story creation — where the rubric says "Be unforgiving here" — the gap between "verification method" and "acceptance condition" is the main risk. A story author reading FR-048 with verification "integration" still doesn't know what integration test proves "relevant exceptions" were detected.

The UJs partially compensate — each carries a "Successful outcome" and (where relevant) a "Failure outcome" that function as acceptance scenarios. Section 4.1.2 is exemplary: eight explicit completion conditions for the first live slice, including "confirm through automated tests that private details of the shared topic cannot reach the shared response or payload" (line 112). But that rigor is concentrated in the release-boundary sections, not carried consistently into the FR list.

### Findings

- **[high]** "Relevant" is unbounded in thesis-level FRs (FR-048 §13.6, FR-063 §13.8) — FR-048 ("detect relevant exceptions") and FR-063 ("translate relevant state and actions into household language") are load-bearing for the product thesis but neither defines what counts as "relevant," for whom, or by what test. A story author cannot write an acceptance test for "relevant exceptions detected" without inventing the definition. *Fix:* Add a one-line definition per FR, e.g., for FR-048: "Relevant exception: a calendar, durable-fact, or integration-state change that affects a confirmed household fact, an adult's schedule, or a child's routine, as determined by the context-assembly rules in §12.2."

- **[medium]** "Personality and interaction preferences" undefined (FR-012 §13.2) — "configure personality and interaction preferences" names no configurable dimensions, no persistence target, and no test. The verification is "integration" but what integration test proves a preference was configured? *Fix:* Enumerate the configurable dimensions (e.g., name, tone, verbosity, proactivity default) or cross-reference a UX spec section that does.

- **[medium]** "Distinguish" has no observable surface (FR-022 §13.3) — "Kinward shall distinguish confirmed facts, inferred observations, transient context, and household-shared facts" doesn't say whether the distinction is visible to the user, in the data model, or both. FR-043 ("Users shall inspect why an item appeared, source categories, confidence, sharing class") partially covers the user-facing side, but FR-022's scope is ambiguous. *Fix:* State whether "distinguish" means "store with a typed classification" (data model) or "present visibly differently" (UI), or both.

- **[low]** FR-008 reviewable fields unspecified (§13.1) — "review and correct their profile information" doesn't list which fields. *Fix:* Cross-reference the profile field list or state "at minimum: display name, household role label, and sharing defaults."

## Scope honesty — adequate

Omissions are explicit. Section 4.4 ("Explicit exclusions") lists ten things Kinward excludes — "multi-household tenancy in one deployment," "SaaS control-plane behavior," "billing and commercial entitlements," "support-operator access," "mandatory external memory, knowledge, Home Assistant, or cloud inference services," "routine-centric product behavior," "permanent legacy frontend code," "arbitrary AI-generated client code," "administrator access to another adult's private content merely because of role," "covert monitoring or hidden disclosure of household members' private assistant activity" (lines 153–164). Section 4.3 ("Later scope") defers email, voice, layout editors, emergency mode, maintenance recall, progressive onboarding, tablet workspaces, and native Android. De-scoping is honest, not silent.

Inline milestone callouts serve as `[NON-GOAL for MVP]` tags. FR-045 ("Nudge, interruption, and autonomous-action delivery are Milestone D scope and shall remain disabled until Milestone D delivers them"), FR-047 ("Milestone D"), FR-050 ("Coordination requests are Milestone D scope"), and FR-051 ("Coordination requests are Milestone D scope") all mark deferred scope inline on the requirement. This is clear and travels with the FR.

The gap is the absence of `[ASSUMPTION: …]` tags and an Assumptions Index. The PRD makes inferences that the user didn't directly confirm — for example, Section 3.6 asserts "Live personal tablet and voice surfaces remain required product directions but are not required for the first usable release" without an assumption tag, and Section 14.1's backup inclusion list ("encrypted credential material where supported") makes an assumption about what encryption the architecture will support. These are reasonable inferences, but they're not tagged as assumptions, and the rubric expects an Assumptions Index roundtrip. The decision registers (§18–§19) capture *decisions* (choices to be made) but not *assumptions* (inferences made without confirmation) — the two are distinct, and the PRD has the former but not the latter.

Open-items density is high but honestly flagged. Twenty-two open decisions on a green-light-to-build PRD would normally be a blocker per the rubric. But each carries a safe interim behavior and the readiness criteria (§20) explicitly accept open items "with its owner, due milestone, and safe interim behavior." The risk is manageable because no decision is silently decided — but the density is real.

### Findings

- **[medium]** No `[ASSUMPTION]` tags or Assumptions Index (entire PRD) — The PRD makes product inferences (e.g., §3.6 "Live personal tablet and voice surfaces remain required product directions but are not required for the first usable release"; §14.1 "encrypted credential material where supported") that are not tagged as assumptions and not indexed. An epic author has no way to tell which statements are confirmed vs. inferred. *Fix:* Tag inferences with `[ASSUMPTION: …]` inline and add an Assumptions Index before §20, or explicitly state that all inferences are captured in §18–§19 decision registers.

- **[low]** Section 3.6 surface-classification is asserted, not decided (§3.6) — "Live personal tablet and voice surfaces remain required product directions but are not required for the first usable release" is a product scoping decision presented as a fact, with no PD ID. It should be PD-08 or cross-reference an existing PD. *Fix:* Add a product decision row for "which surfaces are required for the first usable release vs. deferred" if not already covered by an existing PD.

## Downstream usability — adequate

This is a chain-top PRD — downstream usability matters most, per the rubric. The infrastructure for clean source-extraction is strong: IDs are contiguous (FR-001–FR-086, NFR-001–NFR-037, UJ-1–UJ-9, AD-01–AD-15, PD-01–PD-07, INV-1–INV-11), cross-references resolve (every "Section 8.2," "Section 9.2," "Section 10," "Section 11," "Section 12.1/12.3/12.4," "Section 14.3," "Section 4.1.1/4.1.2," "Section 7," "Section 6.5" points to a real section), and the per-requirement traceability table (§17.4) gives every FR and NFR a source, journey, milestone, invariant, verification method, and owner. An architect or story author can extract a single FR's full context from one table row.

The glossary (§3) covers the core domain nouns: Personal assistant, Specialist assistant, Household fallback assistant, Topic, Durable fact, Surface, Meaningful external action. Most are used consistently. But three UJs lack named protagonists — the rubric says "UJs each have a named protagonist; no floating UJs."

- **UJ-4** (line 197): "A connected calendar adds an early-release event associated with a child profile. Kinward compares the event with confirmed household transportation facts and permitted adult calendars." No protagonist is named. Which adult receives the briefing? Is the child named?
- **UJ-7** (line 218): "A child account asks for homework help and later asks the assistant to send a message to a teacher." The child is unnamed. Section 5 opens with "All names below are fictional test personas" (line 168) — but three journeys don't name one.
- **UJ-8** (line 224): "The memory provider or Home Assistant is offline." This is an operational scenario; a named protagonist is less critical but the journey still has no person experiencing the degradation.

UJ-7's coverage is also thin: Section 17.5 lists "UJ-7: FR-032, NFR-004, supported by FR-007 (privacy class), FR-028 (authentication), FR-052–FR-053 (action authority and activity)." Only two primary requirements for a child-safety journey whose outcome includes "Message sending is blocked or routed to authorized-adult approval" (line 220). There is no FR that specifically says "child message-sending is blocked or routed to adult approval" — it's covered indirectly by FR-052 + Section 11 + Section 7.4. A story author extracting UJ-7 gets two primary FRs and must infer the rest.

### Findings

- **[medium]** Three UJs lack named protagonists (UJ-4 §5, UJ-7 §5, UJ-8 §5) — UJ-4 ("A connected calendar adds..."), UJ-7 ("A child account asks..."), and UJ-8 ("The memory provider or Home Assistant is offline") have no named persona. Section 5 says "All names below are fictional test personas" but these three journeys don't name one. A story author can't source-extract a UJ into a story scenario without a protagonist carrying context. *Fix:* Name a protagonist in each — e.g., UJ-4: "Jordan's child Sam has a calendar event..."; UJ-7: "Sam, a child account, asks..."; UJ-8: "Alex starts Kinward when..."

- **[medium]** UJ-7 child-safety coverage is thin (§17.5) — UJ-7's outcome includes "Message sending is blocked or routed to authorized-adult approval" and "The child's ordinary conversation is not automatically copied into guardian memory," but the primary mapping is only FR-032 and NFR-004. FR-052–FR-053 are listed as "supported by," not primary. The message-blocking and no-copy-to-guardian-memory behaviors have no dedicated FR. *Fix:* Either add FRs for "child external actions require adult approval by default" and "child conversation is not copied into guardian memory," or promote FR-052–FR-053 to primary in UJ-7's coverage row and state the derivation explicitly.

- **[low]** Glossary drift: "shared surface" vs "shared display" (§3.6, §10, FR-039, FR-044) — Section 3.6 defines "Surface" with classes including "shared kitchen display" and "shared living-room display," but Section 10 is titled "Deterministic shared-surface identity policy" and NFR-003 says "shared-surface state." "Shared surface" (hyphenated) is not in the glossary; "shared display" is a surface class. *Fix:* Add "shared surface" to the glossary as a synonym for the shared-display surface class, or standardize on "shared display" throughout.

## Shape fit — strong

The PRD's shape matches its product type. Kinward is a consumer household product with meaningful UX and multi-person privacy boundaries — the rubric says "Consumer product / multi-stakeholder B2B / meaningful UX → UJs with named protagonists are load-bearing." The PRD has nine UJs (§5), which is appropriate density for a product with administrator, adult, teen, child, and shared-surface-participant roles. The UJs are load-bearing: the traceability table (§17.4) maps each UJ to specific FRs, and Section 17.5's journey coverage summary is explicitly derived from the table. The PRD is not over-formalized (no UJ density inflation for a single-operator tool — this is a multi-person household) and not under-formalized (it has a glossary, traceability, decision registers, and measurable SMs).

The decision-register shape (§18–§19) is appropriate for a chain-top PRD with a solo developer but launch-level rigor — it forces the architect and product owner to name what's unresolved without blocking the entire document. The milestone structure (§16: A→B→C→D→E) with explicit exit gates matches a rebuild-from-salvage context (the PRD's frontmatter cites `docs/pivot/single-household-pivot-and-rebuild-plan.md` and `docs/pivot/salvage-matrix.md`), and the salvage-matrix acceptance rule in Milestone A's exit gate (lines 716) correctly distinguishes retained subsystems from greenfield work — the brownfield shape fit the rubric calls for.

### Findings

- **[low]** No Open Questions section (entire PRD) — The PRD uses §18–§19 decision registers instead of a traditional "Open Questions" section. This is arguably better for a chain-top PRD, but a reader expecting the standard section may not realize the registers serve that function until §20. *Fix:* Add a one-line pointer at the end of §1 or §2: "Open decisions are tracked in §18 (architecture) and §19 (product); see §20 for readiness criteria."
