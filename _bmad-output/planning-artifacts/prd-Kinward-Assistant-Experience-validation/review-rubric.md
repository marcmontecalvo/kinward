# BMAD PRD Rubric Review: Kinward Assistant Experience

## Overall verdict

**Thin; not ready to be finalized or used as the sole basis for implementation planning.** The PRD has a coherent single-household direction, a useful journey/requirement skeleton, and clean requirement numbering, but it leaves release boundaries, blocking decisions, verification criteria, and source traceability unresolved. It also contains one public-repository safety violation that must be corrected before publication or downstream reuse.

## Decision-readiness — Thin

**Judgment:** The document identifies important decisions but incorrectly treats several architecture- and acceptance-defining choices as non-blocking. It does not give decision owners, deadlines, defaults, or an unambiguous release gate, so teams cannot safely convert all Milestone B/C requirements into stories.

**Findings:**

- **High — Open decisions are mislabeled as non-blocking.** Section 12, lines 396-406, says the listed decisions do not block the PRD, yet authentication and recovery, the mandatory model-provider contract, calendar synchronization, storage boundaries, initial identity signals, export/restore guarantees, and proactivity thresholds directly determine acceptance behavior for FR-007/008, FR-016, FR-032/033, FR-052/053, FR-064, and FR-080/082. **Impact:** Architecture and epic authors must invent product policy, and incompatible assumptions can enter implementation before readiness review. **Concrete fix:** Turn Section 12 into a decision register with owner, due milestone, affected requirement IDs, safe default, and blocking status; resolve all items affecting Milestones B and C before those requirements are marked ready.
- **High — The finalization and release gates are ambiguous and partly circular.** Section 8.2, line 305, refers to “the first household production milestone”; Section 13, lines 410-416, refers to “the first milestone” and “the first planned milestone” without naming them, while requiring downstream architecture, epics, stories, and a readiness review before the PRD can be final. **Impact:** Reviewers cannot tell whether PRD completion, implementation readiness, or production readiness is being assessed, or whether Milestone B or C is the gate. **Concrete fix:** Define separate named gates for PRD approval, Milestone B implementation readiness, Milestone C first usable release, and production readiness, each with product-level entry/exit criteria.
- **Medium — Success is stated behaviorally but not operationally measurable.** Section 3.1, lines 37-49, uses terms such as “voluntarily use,” “recurring real needs,” “important,” “before,” and “normal use” without a cohort, observation period, baseline, target, or measurement method. **Impact:** Product outcomes cannot adjudicate release readiness or later tradeoffs, and counter-metrics cannot show regression. **Concrete fix:** Add a measure table with definition, baseline, target, observation window, instrument or study method, and release relevance for every primary measure and counter-metric.

## Substance over theater — Adequate

**Judgment:** Most sections contain real product content rather than ceremonial prose, especially the privacy, action, integration, and surface requirements. Substance is weakened by an unsupported “already accepted” claim and escape-clause wording that makes several requirements look firmer than they are.

**Findings:**

- **High — “Foundation already accepted” is an unsupported status assertion.** Section 9, lines 329-340, declares Milestone A accepted, including provider adapters, a frontend proof of concept, and workflow foundations, but the PRD gives no acceptance evidence or criteria. Its source manifest also points to a non-authoritative migration-status file at line 10, making the likely basis for this claim unavailable within the approved source set. **Impact:** Downstream planning may treat unverified foundations as constraints and skip validation that the pivot plan explicitly requires for salvaged subsystems. **Concrete fix:** Rename the milestone to “Foundation baseline” unless acceptance evidence is linked; for every accepted item, cite an inspectable check and the Kinward contract/test that proves legacy assumptions were removed.
- **Medium — Several FRs use subjective qualifiers or provider escape clauses instead of product behavior.** Section 7.2, line 198, conditions cancellation on what a provider permits; Section 7.3, lines 210-215, uses “relevant,” “consequential,” and “sensitive”; Section 7.7, lines 258 and 267, uses “where practical,” “secure defaults,” and “bounded” without definitions. **Impact:** A technically minimal implementation can claim compliance while omitting cancellation, retaining unsafe context, tightly coupling a provider, or retrying excessively. **Concrete fix:** Define mandatory baseline capabilities and policy categories, and replace each qualifier with a rule, limit, or documented exception process tied to acceptance tests.

## Strategic coherence — Adequate

**Judgment:** The main strategy is strongly aligned with the authoritative direction: one household, personal assistants, web/PWA first, Home Assistant as physical-state authority, optional providers, declarative UI, and separate Kinward Control. Three alignment gaps could nevertheless distort architecture and sequencing.

**Findings:**

- **High — The declared source set is not the authoritative source set.** Front matter Section `sourceDocuments`, lines 6-10, omits `docs/pivot/salvage-matrix.md` and adds `docs/pivot/migration-status.md`, which is not one of the four authoritative documents for this review. **Impact:** Future reviewers and generators can import conclusions from an unapproved source while missing the controlling subsystem migration rules. **Concrete fix:** Replace the migration-status entry with `docs/pivot/salvage-matrix.md`, use consistent repository-relative paths for all four entries, and add a statement that legacy planning is non-authoritative.
- **High — The shared fallback assistant has no explicit domain contract.** Section 7.2, lines 193-203, specifies ownership only for personal assistants; Section 7.3, line 207, mentions “shared assistants” without defining their ownership, fallback role, allowed capabilities, memory boundary, or routing relationship. **Impact:** Architecture may model a shared assistant as personally owned, as a combined household memory, or not at all, undermining a core privacy boundary from the product brief and repository architecture rules. **Concrete fix:** Add contiguous FRs stating that the shared fallback assistant has no personal owner, cannot hold or retrieve unrestricted personal memory, handles only household-safe capabilities, and yields to a resolved personal assistant when policy allows.
- **High — The first vertical-slice sequence does not implement the authoritative cross-surface validation shape.** Section 9, lines 342-360, limits Milestone B’s live surface to personal mobile and delays shared-display behavior until Milestone C; tablet and desktop have no explicit vertical-slice deliverable. This conflicts with the UX specification’s initial cross-surface slice and the pivot plan’s requirement to render the same capabilities across mobile, tablet, desktop, shared kitchen, and shared living-room displays; Section 10, line 381, says UX remains authoritative unless explicitly narrowed. **Impact:** The surface, privacy, card, and layout architecture could be validated only after substantial personal-assistant work has already coupled to a mobile-specific shape. **Concrete fix:** Either restore a foundation slice across all five named surfaces before the live assistant slice, or explicitly document and justify a narrower sequence while retaining test fixtures for all surface contexts.

## Done-ness clarity — Thin

**Judgment:** IDs make requirements addressable, but many requirements do not define observable pass/fail boundaries. The PRD delegates acceptance criteria to later stories instead of defining enough product acceptance behavior for those stories to be derived consistently.

**Findings:**

- **High — Performance and interaction NFRs lack measurable bounds.** Section 8.3, lines 307-312, requires content to render without waiting and requests to be acknowledged “promptly,” while privacy transitions remove content “immediately”; Section 8.4, lines 314-319, requires room-distance readability and “appropriately large” targets. **Impact:** Test environments, percentiles, timing thresholds, target sizes, and failure budgets are undefined, so all implementations can pass by interpretation. **Concrete fix:** Specify supported hardware/network conditions and quantitative bounds, for example p95 locally available home render, p95 input acknowledgment, maximum private-content removal latency, minimum touch target size, and a defined viewing-distance/type-size test.
- **High — Product acceptance is deferred rather than specified.** Section 13, lines 412-415, asks future epics to map requirements and future stories to create testable acceptance criteria, but the PRD itself contains no requirement-to-verification matrix or acceptance examples for the highest-risk flows. **Impact:** Epic writers become de facto product-policy authors, especially for privacy, memory promotion, approvals, degraded operation, backup, and restore. **Concrete fix:** Add product-level acceptance criteria or verification notes for each FR/NFR, with scenario matrices for high-risk requirements; stories may refine implementation detail but must not invent the pass condition.
- **Medium — Privacy and action coverage names states but not invariant outcomes.** Section 8.1, lines 290-297, requires automated tests for recognized, unknown, multiple-person, timed-out, and reduced-confidence shared-surface states but does not state what each state may reveal; Section 7.6, lines 251-254, defines authority and activity fields without a transition or failure matrix. **Impact:** Tests can cover every named state while still allowing inconsistent disclosure, approval bypass, or misleading activity status. **Concrete fix:** Add a privacy decision table and action state machine specifying permitted disclosure, handoff behavior, approval requirement, terminal states, retry/duplicate behavior, and required activity record for every branch.

## Scope honesty — Thin

**Judgment:** The document honestly identifies many later and excluded capabilities, but the “initial usable product” is not tied to a named milestone and later requirements remain mixed into one untagged FR list. Several initial commitments depend on unresolved definitions.

**Findings:**

- **High — The initial release boundary cannot be reconstructed deterministically.** Section 5.1, lines 90-106, defines an “initial usable product,” Section 5.2, lines 108-119, defers capabilities, and Section 9, lines 329-376, defines five milestones without saying which milestone satisfies that initial scope. Requirements such as targeted onboarding (line 189), voice surface support (line 230), and email privacy (line 263) appear in the same undifferentiated FR set despite later-scope placement. **Impact:** Estimation, epic slicing, and readiness review can each select a different MVP while still citing the PRD. **Concrete fix:** Name Milestone C or another explicit milestone as the first usable release, tag every FR/NFR with its required milestone, and distinguish interface/schema readiness from user-visible capability delivery.
- **High — Household data protection is initial scope while its contract remains open.** Section 5.1, line 106, commits backup, restore, and upgrade procedures; Section 7.9, lines 283-285, requires restore and export/import; Section 12, lines 403-405, leaves storage boundaries, export format, and restore guarantee undecided. **Impact:** The first household deployment can accumulate irreplaceable state before the team knows what is backed up, portable, or recoverable. **Concrete fix:** Define the authoritative data inventory, excluded/regenerable data, export schema and versioning, credential treatment, RPO/RTO or equivalent household guarantees, and a clean-deployment restore acceptance test before calling the initial product usable.
- **Medium — “Kinward Control foundations” conceals a broad administration scope.** Section 5.1, line 105, includes foundations for a limited list, while Section 7.8, lines 269-276, requires management of surfaces, layouts, proactivity, filters, and personal integrations; Section 5.2, line 115, defers visual and declarative layout editors. **Impact:** Teams cannot tell whether Milestone C needs read-only visibility, basic forms, full CRUD, or editing workflows, creating hidden schedule and UX scope. **Concrete fix:** Replace “foundations” with an explicit initial capability matrix per Control area and mark every operation read, create, update, delete, preview, or deferred.

## Downstream usability — Thin

**Judgment:** The PRD is navigable and requirements are addressable, but architecture, UX, epics, tests, and safety review still need to infer traceability, canonical vocabulary, release assignment, and persona coverage. One journey-fixture problem makes direct downstream reuse unsafe.

**Findings:**

- **High — Requirements have no source, journey, risk, milestone, or verification traceability.** Section 1, line 22, claims the PRD translates sources into stable testable requirements, but the FR/NFR catalog at lines 176-327 and milestones at lines 329-376 contain no mapping. **Impact:** Teams cannot prove source coverage, detect orphan requirements, identify which tests satisfy a requirement, or assess change impact without re-reading all planning documents. **Concrete fix:** Add a traceability table for every FR/NFR with authoritative source section, user journey, target milestone, related risk, and planned verification level.
- **Critical — Journey fixtures use apparent real household identities and still fail complete protagonist coverage.** Section 6 uses “Marc” and “Lisa” repeatedly at lines 136-172; “Marc” matches the named author at line 15, so these are not obviously fictional public-repository examples. UJ-4 at lines 152-156 has no named person, and no journey gives the teen or child persona a protagonist. **Impact:** The PRD violates the repository rule against real household names, propagates sensitive fixture data into stories/tests, and leaves age-specific privacy requirements without a concrete end-to-end scenario. **Concrete fix:** Replace all household names with clearly labeled fictional names, give every journey a named fictional protagonist and role, and add focused teen and child journeys covering age policy, privacy, and account/no-account behavior.
- **Medium — Canonical domain terms and policy predicates are not defined.** The PRD alternates among household member, user, person, current person, owner, participant, audience, and account-bearing member; examples include Sections 4 and 7 at lines 62-86, 193-207, and 217-226. It also leaves “meaningful action,” “durable fact,” “observation,” “topic,” “surface,” “privacy state,” and “identity confidence” without a glossary-level definition. **Impact:** Schemas, APIs, UI copy, authorization policy, and tests can assign different semantics to the same concept. **Concrete fix:** Add a normative glossary and domain relationship summary, select one canonical term per entity/state, define policy predicates, and identify UI-only synonyms separately.

## Shape fit — Adequate

**Judgment:** The document has the expected PRD shape: purpose, outcomes, users, scope, journeys, contiguous requirements, NFRs, milestones, dependencies, risks, decisions, and completion criteria. Its principal shape defect is that assumptions, decisions, and downstream readiness gates are conflated.

**Findings:**

- **Medium — Assumptions and open items are not managed as first-class PRD artifacts.** Section 10, lines 378-384, mixes constraints with prerequisites; Section 12, lines 396-406, lists decisions without owner, status, due point, affected scope, or interim assumption; Section 13, lines 408-416, then relies on downstream artifacts to close the PRD. **Impact:** A reader cannot distinguish a fixed product constraint from a provisional assumption or know which unresolved item invalidates a requirement. **Concrete fix:** Separate constraints, assumptions, dependencies, and decisions; give assumptions validation conditions and expiry points, and give decisions owner/status/due milestone/affected IDs before using downstream readiness as a gate.

## Mechanical notes

- The full PRD and all four specified authoritative source documents were reviewed; no legacy planning document was used.
- FR IDs are contiguous and unique from `FR-001` through `FR-083`.
- NFR IDs are contiguous and unique from `NFR-001` through `NFR-024`.
- Journey IDs are contiguous and unique from `UJ-1` through `UJ-7`.
- Numeric IDs are syntactically stable, but stability in downstream work is not yet demonstrated because there is no traceability or deprecation/change policy.
- The PRD has no normative glossary, assumptions register, requirement-level milestone tags, or requirement-to-source/verification matrix.
- Severity totals: **critical 1, high 11, medium 6, low 0**.
